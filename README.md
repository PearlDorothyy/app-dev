## Lolo and the Kid
![image](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/56cf89f0-303a-4120-8662-0919cb3891d9)

###OVERVIEW
>**Lolo and the Kid** is a weeper of a capital T thorough and to the point. It's all about a old man *(Lolo Mario)* exploiting his grandson out of sheer helplessness. He exploits the kid for survival and finds some comfort in the company of the kid. The relationship isn't purely transactional. Lolo, though, starts noticing the extent to which a major chunk of their relation is transactional. It is at the expense of the better development of the child that someone else can provide. Lolo, though, is unable to reconcile with their forthcoming detachment. The movie is about themes of a constructed family, unwanted sections of society, parenthood, and survival in the present day. It presents Lolo's battle to abandon his emotional demands and prioritize Kid's expectations over his own. With all of these trappings, it becomes a foreordained tearjerker full of cliches. The film largely counts on sentimentality and the perceived cuteness of its central relationship. While a good-hearted drama about the requirement to relate with other human beings, it is extremely forgettable.

**Movie Lesson**
>This movie offers a life-changing lesson, showing us that it's not just about an elder teaching a bad habit to their grandchild. It gives us hope that it's okay to ask for help, even if we're used to doing things a certain way. It emphasizes the importance of letting go of ingrained habits to help ourselves and those around us. Like Lolo Mario letting go of the Kid, the film suggests that sometimes releasing our hold on familiar patterns is necessary to allow others to thrive and reach their full potential.

>The movie also underscores the importance of gratitude and remembering our roots. As seen in the Kid's unwavering remembrance of Lolo Mario, despite not being blood relatives, the movie emphasizes cherishing the sacrifices made for us. This serves as a reminder to *love and appreciate those around us, especially our elders—grandparents and parents—who give us reason to live.*
